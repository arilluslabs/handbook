Engineering & QA is responsible for everything we build and the quality of what we ship. Engineers design, implement and maintain the software; QA engineers own test strategy, coverage and release confidence. Both disciplines work shoulder-to-shoulder on every project — quality is not a handoff at the end, it's a shared standard throughout.

The function is led by **Anand** (Partner, Engineering & Technology).

## Software Engineering Ladder

Each level assumes everything in the levels below it.

### L1 — Associate Software Engineer

*Scope: a task (days)*

- Completes well-defined tasks with close guidance from a senior
- Writes working code that passes review; reviews frequently catch significant issues
- Learns the codebase, tooling, and team conventions rather than imposing habits from elsewhere
- Asks for help early rather than staying stuck — "stuck for more than an hour, ask" is the rule
- Treats code review feedback as learning, not criticism

### L2 — Software Engineer

*Scope: a feature (1–2 weeks)*

- Delivers features end-to-end from a written story — including tests, edge cases, and error handling — with reviews catching only minor issues
- Debugs own problems methodically before escalating
- Estimates own work with reasonable accuracy; flags scope changes early
- Starts reviewing L1 code and giving useful, specific feedback
- Communicates progress without needing to be chased

### L3 — Senior Software Engineer

*Scope: a project's technical delivery (months)*

- Owns a module or workstream end-to-end: design, implementation, testing and handover
- Designs solutions before implementing — considers edge cases, performance and security at the start, not after the fact
- Breaks down ambiguous requirements into concrete technical tasks and stories
- Reviews code as a routine, not an exception; mentors L1–L2 engineers actively
- Can **Tech Lead a standard project**: runs technical standups, unblocks teammates, owns the technical relationship with the project coordinator and client
- Handles straightforward technical conversations with clients directly
- Raises concerns about scope, feasibility or quality early — not at the end of a sprint

> The L2 → L3 transition is the most consequential promotion. It means the engineer can be trusted with a project's technical delivery without a senior above them. Client-facing ability is required at L3; an engineer who cannot represent the work to a client caps here regardless of technical depth.

### L4 — Lead Software Engineer

*Scope: multiple workstreams or the hardest single projects*

- Tech-leads complex or large projects; owns architecture decisions within them
- Acts as **fractional Architect/Reviewer** for projects led by L3s — reviews approach at kickoff, checks in at major milestones
- Handles difficult technical client conversations independently: pushback, trade-offs, "can we do this in half the time?"
- Raises the team's engineering bar through documented standards and patterns, not just personal output
- Identifies systemic problems across projects and drives solutions, not just workarounds

### L5 — Senior Lead Software Engineer

*Scope: several projects / a sub-practice*

- Provides technical oversight across a portfolio of active projects
- Escalation point for the firm's hardest technical problems — the person others go to when they're genuinely stuck
- Owns cross-project decisions: technology choices, reusable components, shared libraries, engineering standards
- First Engineering Manager: owns appraisals and growth plans for L1–L3 engineers
- Measured by the capability of the engineers around them, not just their own output

### L6 — Principal Engineer

*Scope: the firm*

- Owns the engineering practice itself: how Arillus builds software, the stack decisions that span all clients, the hiring bar, the firm's technical reputation
- Trusted by clients as the most senior technical voice in the firm
- Develops and stretches the L4–L5 layer
- Measured on the long-term capability and quality of the whole engineering organisation

## QA Ladder

### L1 — Associate QA Engineer

*Scope: testing tasks within a sprint*

- Executes test plans written by others accurately and thoroughly
- Reports defects with clear steps to reproduce, expected vs. actual behaviour, and environment details
- Learns the project's domain, testing tools, and team conventions
- Asks for help early when a test case is ambiguous or a defect is hard to reproduce

### L2 — QA Engineer

*Scope: a small project's full quality coverage*

- Independently owns testing for a small project: writes test plans from user stories, tracks coverage, manages regression
- Designs and maintains a regression suite for the features they own
- Identifies gaps in requirements and raises them before development begins, not after
- Communicates defect status and release confidence without being prompted

### L3 — Senior QA Engineer

*Scope: a project's quality delivery*

- Defines test strategy from project kickoff: what to automate, what to manual test, where the risk sits
- Reviews L1–L2 test plans and gives specific, constructive feedback
- Leads automation efforts and sets the standard for how the project tests
- Handles quality conversations with clients directly: explains coverage, risk trade-offs, and release decisions
- Raises quality concerns early — "this isn't testable as written" before development starts, not during UAT

### L4 — QA Lead

*Scope: quality across the firm's active projects*

- Sets quality standards across multiple projects and audits adherence
- Reviews test approaches at project kickoff; spot-checks coverage and quality at milestones (fractional)
- Mentors L1–L2 QA engineers; owns their growth
- Identifies systemic quality issues across projects and drives process improvements firm-wide
